# Strategic Principles

Operational principles extracted from books by the knowledge-etl pipeline.
Each principle is attributed to its author and source.

## Donella H. Meadows -- Thinking in Systems
*Source: thinking-in-systems | Extracted: 2026-03-11*

- **[Meadows]** When you identify a leverage point in a system, verify you are pushing it in the correct direction before acting, because intuition about where to intervene is often right but the direction is often backwards. -- *Before applying pressure at a leverage point—cutting a budget, tightening a rule, accelerating a process—explicitly ask: does my intervention oppose the problem dynamic or amplify it? Test the direction with a small change or model before committing.* (_Chapter Six: Leverage Points—Places to Intervene in a System_)
- **[Meadows]** Before rebuilding physical infrastructure to fix a system problem, first look for missing or delayed information flows, because restoring feedback is usually faster, cheaper, and more effective. -- *When diagnosing a persistent system malfunction, map what information reaches each decision-maker, when it arrives, and how accurately it reflects system state. Restore or add missing feedback loops before pursuing structural or physical fixes.* (_Chapter Six: Leverage Points—Places to Intervene in a System_)
- **[Meadows]** When a system persistently produces bad outcomes, redesign the information, incentives, and constraints acting on decision-makers rather than replacing the people in those roles. -- *When faced with chronic poor performance—whether in an organization, policy, or institution—resist the impulse to fire, elect, or appoint new individuals. Instead, ask what information is unavailable to the actors, what incentives are misaligned, and what constraints are absent, then change those structural features.* (_Chapter 4: Why Systems Surprise Us_)

## Matthew Skelton & Manuel Pais -- Team Topologies: Organizing Business and Technology Teams for Fast Flow
*Source: team-topologies | Extracted: 2026-03-11*

- **[Skelton & Pais]** Design your team structure to match your desired software architecture before writing the code, because organizational communication patterns will override any architecture you mandate afterward. -- *When planning a new system or restructuring an existing one, first diagram the software architecture you want, then reshape team boundaries and reporting lines to mirror it — not the other way around. If your teams are organized in functional silos (QA, DBA, front-end, back-end), expect siloed, tightly-coupled software regardless of what the architecture document says.* (_Chapter 2: Conway's Law and Why It Matters_)
- **[Skelton & Pais]** Switch deliberately between close team collaboration and X-as-a-Service based on whether you need rapid discovery or predictable delivery — not based on habit or org chart proximity. -- *When a team is exploring new technology, entering a new domain, or defining API boundaries, put it in explicit collaboration mode with the relevant team for a bounded period. Once the interface is stable and the approach is understood, transition to X-as-a-Service so each team can deliver independently with lower cognitive overhead. Treat ongoing collaboration on a stable interface as a signal that the boundary is wrong, not that the teams need more communication.* (_Chapter 8: Evolve Team Structures with Organizational Sensing_)
